  ## About Water Availability
  The global water cycle is critical for providing freshwater for human consumption, generating electricity, sustaining agriculture through irrigation, and supporting ecosystems. However, reliably estimating water availability is challenging because water on the land exists in various forms, from solid (snow and glaciers) to liquid (rainfall) and is stored in different compartments, from underground in aquifers up to the surface in lakes, rivers, and soil. Driven by natural forces like weather as well as human activities, water is either stored or moves between these compartments; quantifying these processes is key to understanding water availability.  NASA Water Insight combines satellite data and models to measure water availability in key storage components such as snow, groundwater, surface water, and soil moisture in near real time so water managers can make informed decisions.

  The NASA Water Insight team is building a state-of-the-art modeling system to improve estimates of water availability over North and Central America. The team  combines observations from NASA satellites with modeling to provide daily estimates of the variables described above, like groundwater, snow, and soil moisture, and includes data going back to 2002. Managers can also use this system to  estimate  hazards such as [droughts](https://deploy-preview-2--water-insight.netlify.app/themes/droughts) and [floods](https://deploy-preview-2--water-insight.netlify.app/themes/floods), both of which can be costly for agriculture and human infrastructure. The new modeling system is called the North American Land Data Assimilation System version 3 (NLDAS-3). Links to additional information can be found below under Resources.
