  ## About Floods
  Flooding is the costliest U.S. natural disaster, causing billions of dollars in damages annually. Floods are natural events that occur when water overflows onto land that is normally dry, often resulting from excessive rainfall, rapid snowmelt, storm surges, or the failure of natural or man-made barriers like levees or dams. Floods can be classified into three main types based on their origin: pluvial, fluvial, and coastal. Pluvial floods occur due to intense rainfall overwhelming local drainage systems or accumulating on the surface, especially in urban areas. Fluvial floods happen when rivers or streams overflow their banks, usually after prolonged or heavy rainfall or rapid snowmelt upstream. Coastal floods are caused by storm surges, high tides, or tsunamis pushing seawater onto land, often exacerbated by strong winds and low atmospheric pressure. The widespread and repeated nature of flood events, especially in urban and coastal areas where infrastructure and property are densely concentrated, contributes to the significant impact of flooding on life, livelihood, and property in the U.S. 
  
  The Water Insight team is building the state-of-the-art North American Land Data Assimilation System Phase 3 (NLDAS-3) modeling system to produce improved estimates of variables relevant to flood monitoring, including soil moisture, surface runoff, flooded fraction, and surface water elevation. In particular, NLDAS-3 incorporates the Hydrological Modeling and Analysis Platform (HYMAP) global hydrodynamic model to provide detailed information about pluvial, fluvial, and coastal flooding across Central and North America. NLDAS-3 will adopt approaches similar to those recently  used to assess compound coastal flood risk in both southern Louisiana and the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna River Delta, and currently being used along the Chesapeake Bay. At a finer spatial scale, HyMAP has also been used to monitor urban flooding in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.
