  ## About Droughts
  Drought is an atmospheric or hydrologic process referring to a period of time when a region is drier than normal. Ecosystems are adapted to expect an amount of precipitation that is unique to the geography and weather patterns of their region, so when actual rainfall amounts aren’t sufficient to meet the demand, the consequence can be vegetation and crop stress, diminished water storage in reservoirs and aquifers, and greater susceptibility to wildfires. As such, it is important to understand and be able to predict the severity of droughts in order to efficiently allocate resources when drought does occur. NOAA’s National Integrated Drought Information System (NIDIS) is an [integrated information system](https://www.drought.gov/about/integrated-information-systems) that coordinates drought monitoring, forecasting, planning, and information at federal, tribal, state, and local levels across the country. Each week, the [U.S. Drought Monitor (USDM)](https://droughtmonitor.unl.edu/) is released, showing the current drought conditions across the United States. The USDM has been a team effort since its inception in 1999, produced jointly by the National Drought Mitigation Center at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) and the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). 
  
  The USDM employs a convergence of evidence approach, bringing together weather and hydrological data with records of drought impacts, on-the-ground observations and local expert feedback.There are many ways to measure drought, reflecting its multifaceted impact on the ecosystem. This includes meteorological drought, which describes the current deviation from the normal amount of rainfall, agricultural drought, which considers the actual amount of water available for plants in the soil given the amount of precipitation and evapotranspiration, and hydrological drought, which characterizes the effect on lakes, reservoirs, and aquifers. Since there is a wide variety between regions in what is considered “normal” precipitation, soil moisture, and water storage amount, drought is usually measured in terms of the current position of a drought metric in the distribution of historical values for each locale. For example, the Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI) calculates how many standard deviations the recent amount of rainfall is away from the local mean value, and soil moisture percentiles describe the percentage of time in the known history that less water is available for vegetation.

  Over the past decade, numerous new drought-related datasets have been developed that have been integrated into the operational decision-making process by the USDM. These include drought metrics from advanced land surface model simulations that can provide spatial and temporal information over areas that aren’t covered by ground-based observational networks. The long-term data record, quality meteorological inputs and soil physics, and near real-time forecasting capability of land surface modeling products from NLDAS-2 and SPoRT-LIS datasets have provided the information that agriculturalists, policy makers, and the general public need to evaluate drought and take action to mitigate the negative effects. The upcoming release of the NLDAS-3 data system will improve upon this legacy by producing data at an unprecedented 1-km resolution and by integrating an improved system for modeling vegetation and groundwater storage, which can increase the information quality and reduce the cost of managing resources during periods of drought.
