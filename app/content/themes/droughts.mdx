  ## About Droughts
  Drought is a climate process referring to a period of time when a region is drier than normal. Ecosystems are adapted to expect an amount of precipitation that is unique to the geography and weather patterns of their region, so when actual rainfall amounts don’t meet the mark, the consequence can be vegetation and crop stress, diminished water storage in reservoirs and aquifers, and greater susceptibility to wildfires. As such, it is important to understand and be able to predict the severity of droughts in order to efficiently allocate resources when drought occurs. There are many ways to measure drought, reflecting its multifaceted impact on the ecosystem. This includes meteorological drought, which describes the current deviation from the normal amount of rainfall, agricultural drought, which considers the actual amount of water available for plants in the soil given the amount of precipitation and evapotranspiration, and hydrological drought, which characterizes the effect on lakes, reservoirs, and aquifers. Since there is a wide variety between regions in what is considered “normal” precipitation, soil moisture, and water storage amount, drought is usually measured in terms of the current position of a drought metric in the distribution of historical values for each locale. For example, the Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI) calculates how many standard deviations the recent amount of rainfall is away from the local mean value, and soil moisture percentiles describe the percentage of time in the known history of the local climate that less water is available for vegetation.

  The long-term data record, quality meteorological inputs and soil physics, and near real-time forecasting capability of the NLDAS-2 and SPoRT-LIS datasets have a storied record of providing the information that agriculturalists, policy makers, and the general public need to evaluate drought and take action to mitigate the negative effects. The upcoming release of the NLDAS-3 data system will carry and improve upon this legacy by producing data at an unprecedented 1km resolution, and integrating an improved system for modeling vegetation and groundwater storage, which can increase the information quality and reduce the cost of managing resources during periods of drought.
